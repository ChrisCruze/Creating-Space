## Method: KonMari Framework

Following Marie Kondo's method, decluttering proceeds by category (not location) in this order:

1. **Clothes** (including underwear, outerwear, jewelry, accessories, bags)
2. **Books**
3. **Papers**
4. **Komono (Miscellaneous Items)** - kitchen items, bathroom products, cleaning supplies, tools, electronics, home decor, craft supplies
5. **Sentimental Items** (last, hardest)

**Core Principles**:
- Tidy by category, NOT by location
- Gather everything from that category from entire home before deciding
- Hold each item, use physical touch to assess connection
- Ask "Does this spark joy?" - pay attention to body response
- Thank items before discarding (gratitude vs. guilt mindset)
- Discard first completely, THEN organize what remains

## Documentation

This repository includes a comprehensive documentation system based on the **Two-System Approach to Sustainable Decluttering**. The framework separates behavior (time) from inventory (space) to create maintainable, stress-proof systems.

### The Two-System Framework

The core insight: **behavior and inventory serve different cognitive purposes**. When combined, they create confusion and overwhelm. When separated, each system stays focused, readable, and scalable.

**System One: Operating System** - Defines *what you do and how often* (behavior, cadence, time)  
**System Two: Inventory Map** - Defines *what you own and where it lives* (stuff, space, truth)

Together, they create a closed loop: habits prevent clutter, inventory prevents forgetfulness, and decision rules prevent regression.

## Minimalist Decluttering Research (January 4, 2026)

**Manhattan-Specific Decluttering Strategies**

*Key Principles for Small NYC Apartments:*
- Maximize vertical space with shelves, wall-mounted organizers, and stackable storage
- Multi-functional furniture essential: storage ottomans, benches with hidden compartments, under-bed storage, coffee tables with built-in drawers
- "One in, one out" rule: when buying something new, donate/discard one (or two) similar items
- Clear surfaces principle: keep countertops, tables, desks minimal - only daily essentials visible
- Daily tidying ritual: 5-15 minutes end-of-day reset returning items to designated homes
- Designated drop-off zone near entrance: small table for keys, mail, bags to contain clutter spread
- Smart storage solutions: slim hangers, clear bins for visibility, labeled boxes, over-the-door organizers

*Manhattan Storage Solutions:*
- Floating shelves maximize vertical space without furniture bulk
- Recessed shelves utilize wall depth
- Under-bed drawers/bins double storage without floor space
- Multi-compartment organizers and drawer dividers
- Wall-mounted hooks for accessories, coats
- Baskets to corral everyday items visually
- Hidden storage in furniture pieces

*NYC-Specific Challenges:*
- Small closets in pre-war and older buildings
- Limited square footage requires every item to earn its place
- Clutter accumulates faster in small spaces
- Storage solutions must be creative and vertical-focused

**The KonMari Method Framework**

*Five Categories in Order (easiest to hardest):*
1. **Clothes** (including underwear, outerwear, jewelry, accessories, bags)
   - Gather ALL clothes from entire house including out-of-season
   - Touch each item, ask "Does this spark joy?"
   - Fold vertically in rectangles that stand up (not stacked flat)
   - Downgrading to "loungewear" is discouraged
   - Start with off-season clothing for more objective decisions

2. **Books**
   - Assemble all books from entire house
   - Touch to "wake them up" but don't open (prevents false joy)
   - Question: "Will this book help me on my journey ahead?" not "Is this a good book?"
   - Keep only what sparks joy or serves current goals

3. **Papers**
   - Goal: radical reduction, very little paper should remain
   - Seminar/study papers: discard once course ends (value was attending, not papers)
   - Exception: may need course materials for later exams
   - Categorize, then organize by category and alphabetically if keeping

4. **Komono (Miscellaneous Items)**
   - Everything else except sentimental: kitchen items, bathroom products, cleaning supplies, tools, electronics, home decor, craft supplies, etc.
   - Gather by sub-category, see everything at once
   - Identifies duplicates, unusable items, things kept "just because"
   - Most difficult category due to volume and shared household items

5. **Sentimental Items** (last, hardest)
   - Photos, mementos, heirlooms
   - Take photos out of albums, go through one by one
   - Keep only what truly sparks joy
   - By this point, decision-making skills are honed

*Core KonMari Principles:*
- Tidy by category, NOT by location (prevents repeating work in multiple rooms)
- Gather everything from that category from entire home before deciding
- See total volume to understand scope and make smart decisions
- Hold each item, use physical touch to assess connection
- Ask "Does this spark joy?" - pay attention to body response
- Thank items before discarding (shifts mindset from guilt to gratitude)
- Gifts that don't spark joy: have already fulfilled purpose in moment received
- Discard first completely, THEN organize what remains
- Empty electronics packages and spare buttons: will never need them
- Maintain gratitude mindset throughout process

**Practical Decluttering Methods**

*The SOS Method (Simplify, Organize, Systematize):*
- Simplify: Declutter to core - keep what you use and love
- Organize: Assign logical home to everything remaining
- Systematize: Implement boundaries and habits to maintain

*The 4-Box Method:*
- Keep box: items staying in current location
- Donate/sell box: good condition items for others
- Store box: seasonal or rarely-used items with purpose
- Trash/recycle box: broken, unusable, expired items

*15-Minute Wins (#15minwin):*
- Set timer for 15 minutes focused decluttering
- One drawer, shelf, or small area per session
- No phone checking or distractions during timer
- Makes significant impact in small spaces without overwhelm

*Snowball Method:*
- Day 1: discard 1 item
- Day 2: discard 2 items
- Day 3: discard 3 items
- Continue for 30 days
- Works well with accountability buddy via photos

*Timeline Planning:*
- One-month plan: 20 minutes daily on small zones + weekend blocks for bigger areas
- Two-month plan: one room/category per week
- Three-month plan: one area every 1-2 weeks depending on size
- Six-month plan: two rooms/categories per month
- One-year plan: one room/category per month

## Minimalist Decluttering Research (January 4, 2026)

**Manhattan-Specific Decluttering Strategies**

*Key Principles for Small NYC Apartments:*
- Maximize vertical space with shelves, wall-mounted organizers, and stackable storage
- Multi-functional furniture essential: storage ottomans, benches with hidden compartments, under-bed storage, coffee tables with built-in drawers
- "One in, one out" rule: when buying something new, donate/discard one (or two) similar items
- Clear surfaces principle: keep countertops, tables, desks minimal - only daily essentials visible
- Daily tidying ritual: 5-15 minutes end-of-day reset returning items to designated homes
- Designated drop-off zone near entrance: small table for keys, mail, bags to contain clutter spread
- Smart storage solutions: slim hangers, clear bins for visibility, labeled boxes, over-the-door organizers

*Manhattan Storage Solutions:*
- Floating shelves maximize vertical space without furniture bulk
- Recessed shelves utilize wall depth
- Under-bed drawers/bins double storage without floor space
- Multi-compartment organizers and drawer dividers
- Wall-mounted hooks for accessories, coats
- Baskets to corral everyday items visually
- Hidden storage in furniture pieces

*NYC-Specific Challenges:*
- Small closets in pre-war and older buildings
- Limited square footage requires every item to earn its place
- Clutter accumulates faster in small spaces
- Storage solutions must be creative and vertical-focused

   - Start with off-season clothing for more objective decisions

2. **Books**
   - Assemble all books from entire house
   - Touch to "wake them up" but don't open (prevents false joy)

## Minimalist Decluttering Research (January 4, 2026)

**Manhattan-Specific Decluttering Strategies**

*Key Principles for Small NYC Apartments:*
- Maximize vertical space with shelves, wall-mounted organizers, and stackable storage
- Multi-functional furniture essential: storage ottomans, benches with hidden compartments, under-bed storage, coffee tables with built-in drawers
- "One in, one out" rule: when buying something new, donate/discard one (or two) similar items
- Clear surfaces principle: keep countertops, tables, desks minimal - only daily essentials visible
- Daily tidying ritual: 5-15 minutes end-of-day reset returning items to designated homes
- Designated drop-off zone near entrance: small table for keys, mail, bags to contain clutter spread
- Smart storage solutions: slim hangers, clear bins for visibility, labeled boxes, over-the-door organizers

*Manhattan Storage Solutions:*
- Floating shelves maximize vertical space without furniture bulk
- Recessed shelves utilize wall depth
- Under-bed drawers/bins double storage without floor space
- Multi-compartment organizers and drawer dividers
- Wall-mounted hooks for accessories, coats
- Baskets to corral everyday items visually
- Hidden storage in furniture pieces

*NYC-Specific Challenges:*
- Small closets in pre-war and older buildings
- Limited square footage requires every item to earn its place
- Clutter accumulates faster in small spaces
- Storage solutions must be creative and vertical-focused

**The KonMari Method Framework**

*Five Categories in Order (easiest to hardest):*
1. **Clothes** (including underwear, outerwear, jewelry, accessories, bags)
   - Gather ALL clothes from entire house including out-of-season
   - Touch each item, ask "Does this spark joy?"
   - Fold vertically in rectangles that stand up (not stacked flat)
   - Downgrading to "loungewear" is discouraged
   - Start with off-season clothing for more objective decisions

2. **Books**
   - Assemble all books from entire house
   - Touch to "wake them up" but don't open (prevents false joy)
   - Question: "Will this book help me on my journey ahead?" not "Is this a good book?"
   - Keep only what sparks joy or serves current goals

3. **Papers**
   - Goal: radical reduction, very little paper should remain
   - Seminar/study papers: discard once course ends (value was attending, not papers)
   - Exception: may need course materials for later exams
   - Categorize, then organize by category and alphabetically if keeping

4. **Komono (Miscellaneous Items)**
   - Everything else except sentimental: kitchen items, bathroom products, cleaning supplies, tools, electronics, home decor, craft supplies, etc.
   - Gather by sub-category, see everything at once
   - Identifies duplicates, unusable items, things kept "just because"
   - Most difficult category due to volume and shared household items

5. **Sentimental Items** (last, hardest)
   - Photos, mementos, heirlooms
   - Take photos out of albums, go through one by one
   - Keep only what truly sparks joy
   - By this point, decision-making skills are honed

*Core KonMari Principles:*
- Tidy by category, NOT by location (prevents repeating work in multiple rooms)
- Gather everything from that category from entire home before deciding
- See total volume to understand scope and make smart decisions
- Hold each item, use physical touch to assess connection
- Ask "Does this spark joy?" - pay attention to body response
- Thank items before discarding (shifts mindset from guilt to gratitude)
- Gifts that don't spark joy: have already fulfilled purpose in moment received
- Discard first completely, THEN organize what remains
- Empty electronics packages and spare buttons: will never need them
- Maintain gratitude mindset throughout process

**Practical Decluttering Methods**

*The SOS Method (Simplify, Organize, Systematize):*
- Simplify: Declutter to core - keep what you use and love
- Organize: Assign logical home to everything remaining
- Systematize: Implement boundaries and habits to maintain

*The 4-Box Method:*
- Keep box: items staying in current location
- Donate/sell box: good condition items for others
- Store box: seasonal or rarely-used items with purpose
- Trash/recycle box: broken, unusable, expired items

*15-Minute Wins (#15minwin):*
- Set timer for 15 minutes focused decluttering
- One drawer, shelf, or small area per session
- No phone checking or distractions during timer
- Makes significant impact in small spaces without overwhelm

*Snowball Method:*
- Day 1: discard 1 item
- Day 2: discard 2 items
- Day 3: discard 3 items
- Continue for 30 days
- Works well with accountability buddy via photos

*Timeline Planning:*
- One-month plan: 20 minutes daily on small zones + weekend blocks for bigger areas
- Two-month plan: one room/category per week
- Three-month plan: one area every 1-2 weeks depending on size
- Six-month plan: two rooms/categories per month
- One-year plan: one room/category per month

**Common Items to Declutter**

*Kitchen Culprits:*
- Expired pantry items, snacks, baking ingredients
- Flavorless or never-used spices
- Duplicate measuring tools, utensils, spatulas
- Excess coffee mugs, water bottles (keep 2-3 per person)

*Bedroom/Closet:*
- Too small or too large clothing
- Decorative pillows not worth daily effort

*Living Areas:*
- Excess remotes and electronics
- Unsightly cable clutter (needs management)
- Surface clutter accumulation

**Maintaining Decluttered State**

*Daily Habits:*
- Daily tidying: few minutes returning items to homes
- "Tidy as you go": take something when leaving room
- Clear surfaces before bed
- Put items back immediately after use

*Weekly Practices:*
- Dedicated organization day
- Surface sweep and reset
- Category-specific mini-reviews

*Ongoing Systems:*
- "One in, one out" rule strictly enforced
- Designated spaces for everyday items
- Resist impulse purchases (mindful consumption)
- Regular assessments to prevent creep
- Seasonal reassessment of belongings
- No-clutter zones (establish and maintain)

*Decision Framework:*
- "Have I used this recently?"
- "Does this make my life better?"
- "Does this support the life I want to create?"
- "Where will this go?" (before purchasing)

## Purpose

## Key Principles

## Key Takeaways

## Why Splitting Your Systems is the Secret to Sanity

The fundamental insight: **behavior and inventory serve different cognitive purposes**. When you combine them into a single system, you create confusion, overwhelm, and eventual collapse. When you separate them, each system stays focused, readable, and scalable.

### The Core Problem

Most decluttering systems fail because they try to answer two fundamentally different questions in one place:

1. **"What do I do, and how often?"** (Time & Behavior)
2. **"What do I own, and where does it live?"** (Space & Objects)

Conflating time-based actions with spatial inventory creates messy systems that are hard to maintain. Simplicity, clarity, and focused scope are the antidotes to decision fatigue and emotional overwhelm.

## Key Takeaways

- **You're building two documents, not one.** Each document answers a different core question.

## Why Splitting Your Systems is the Secret to Sanity

The fundamental insight: **behavior and inventory serve different cognitive purposes**. When you combine them into a single system, you create confusion, overwhelm, and eventual collapse. When you separate them, each system stays focused, readable, and scalable.

### The Core Problem

Most decluttering systems fail because they try to answer two fundamentally different questions in one place:

1. **"What do I do, and how often?"** (Time & Behavior)
2. **"What do I own, and where does it live?"** (Space & Objects)

Conflating time-based actions with spatial inventory creates messy systems that are hard to maintain. Simplicity, clarity, and focused scope are the antidotes to decision fatigue and emotional overwhelm.

| Aspect | Operating System | Inventory Map |

### 5. How do I make decisions when I'm unsure?

- **"No home = no keep"**: If an item doesn't have a designated space, it goes (unless you create space for it)
- **"6-month storage expiration"**: Items in storage bins get reviewed every 6 months - if unused, they go
- **"Horizontal surface rule"**: Nothing lives permanently on horizontal surfaces except active-use items
- **"Duplicate elimination"**: If you have multiples, keep the best one, donate the rest
- **"Sentimental limit"**: One small box maximum for truly sentimental items

**When Unsure**:
1. Put item in "unresolved" bin (see [[Inventory-Map]])
2. Tag with review date (no more than 3 months out)
3. If still unresolved at review date, it goes

**Success Metric**: Decision-making takes under 2 minutes per item using these rules

## Two-System-Approach

# The Two-System Approach to Sustainable Decluttering

> "When you blur behavior and inventory, you get overwhelm. But when you separate them, everything gets easier."

## Key Takeaways

- **You're building two documents, not one.** Each document answers a different core question.
- **One governs time; the other governs space.** The split keeps your system readable and stress-proof.
- **Together, they create a closed loop of clarity and control.** Habits prevent clutter, inventory prevents forgetfulness, rules prevent regression.

## Why Splitting Your Systems is the Secret to Sanity

The fundamental insight: **behavior and inventory serve different cognitive purposes**. When you combine them into a single system, you create confusion, overwhelm, and eventual collapse. When you separate them, each system stays focused, readable, and scalable.

### The Core Problem

Most decluttering systems fail because they try to answer two fundamentally different questions in one place:

1. **"What do I do, and how often?"** (Time & Behavior)
2. **"What do I own, and where does it live?"** (Space & Objects)

Conflating time-based actions with spatial inventory creates messy systems that are hard to maintain. Simplicity, clarity, and focused scope are the antidotes to decision fatigue and emotional overwhelm.

## The Two Systems

### System One: The Decluttered Space Operating System

**Purpose**: Behavior, Cadence & Time

> "**What do I do, and how often, so my space never quietly degrades again?**"

This document replaces motivation with **defaults**. It prevents regression by defining ongoing maintenance behaviors. See [[Operating-System]] for the complete framework.

**Governs**: Daily habits, weekly routines, monthly reviews, long-term milestones, decision rules

### System Two: The Physical Reality & Inventory Map

**Purpose**: Stuff, Space & Truth

> "**What do I physically have, where can it live, and what is unresolved?**"

This document defines what exists, not what to do. See [[Inventory-Map]] for the complete framework.

**Governs**: Physical containers, assigned items, unresolved items, contextual tagging, location tracking

## Why These Two Systems Must Stay Separate

| Aspect | Operating System | Inventory Map |
|--------|------------------|---------------|
| **Focus** | Time & behavior | Space & objects |
| **Question** | "What do I do?" | "What do I own?" |
| **Updates** | Regular cadence (daily/weekly/monthly) | As items change |
| **Purpose** | Prevent clutter via habits | Prevent forgetfulness |

If you **combine** them:
- You'll build a confusing mega-system
- It will collapse under stress
- Decision-making becomes harder, not easier

If you **separate** them:
- Each system stays focused
- Each system stays readable
- Each system scales independently

## The Closed Loop of Sustainable Decluttering

The two systems work together to create a self-reinforcing cycle:

```
Habits (Operating System) 
  ‚Üí Prevent clutter accumulation
  ‚Üí Inventory stays manageable (Inventory Map)
  ‚Üí Clear decision rules (Operating System)
  ‚Üí Easy to maintain (Inventory Map)
  ‚Üí Habits become automatic (Operating System)
```

### The Three Prevention Mechanisms

| System | Role | Prevents |
|--------|------|----------|
| **Operating System** | Habits & routines | Clutter accumulation |
| **Inventory Map** | Physical tracking | Forgetfulness & re-buying |
| **Decision Rules** | Clear criteria | Emotional backsliding |
